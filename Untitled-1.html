Allama Iqbal

Allama Muhammad Iqbal, born on November 9, 1877, in Sialkot, is celebrated as one of the most influential poets and philosophers of the 20th century. Often referred to as the "Spiritual Father of Pakistan," Iqbal played a crucial role in inspiring the movement for a separate Muslim state in British India. His poetry, primarily written in Urdu and Persian, emphasizes themes of self-awareness, spiritual awakening, and social justice. Notable works such as "Shikwa" and "Jawab-e-Shikwa" articulate the challenges faced by Muslims, urging them to reclaim their identity and potential. In addition to his literary contributions, Iqbal was an advocate for political change. He was instrumental in the Lahore Resolution of 1940, which called for the establishment of Pakistan. His vision encompassed a democratic and progressive society rooted in Islamic values. Iqbal passed away on April 21, 1938, but his legacy endures. His ideas continue to resonate in contemporary discussions of identity and nationalism, making him a central figure in both Pakistani literature and history. Iqbal Day is observed annually on November 9 to honor his contributions.
